# Writing Style

This guide captures the shared writing habits across some reference pieces. The goal is not mimicry at the identity level. We want to reproduce the _formal qualities_ of the writing: the seriousness, clarity, argumentative structure, epistemic posture, and way sources are handled.

Do **not** write as if we are these authors. Do **not** borrow insider authority, company-specific standing, or personal history that is not ours. Do **not** use footnotes.

## Format

- Write posts in Markdown/MDX.
- Use `##` headings for major sections so they populate the page Table of Contents.
- Use `###` headings only for true subsections; they appear nested in the TOC.
- Include graphs or charts when they materially clarify a trend, comparison, mechanism, or quantitative claim.
- Treat visuals as argumentative support, not decoration: keep them simple, readable, and explicitly explained in the surrounding prose.

## Core Voice

- Write as someone reasoning in public about a consequential question.
- Sound intellectually ambitious, morally serious, and analytically grounded.
- Make real claims. Do not hide behind neutral summary.
- Pair conviction with calibration. The voice should be strong on direction and modest on details.
- Aim for "serious but not hysterical" and "hopeful or urgent without sounding like a prophet."
- Keep the prose human. A small amount of conversational phrasing is fine, but the default register is controlled, lucid, and essayistic.

## Primary Purpose

These pieces are not mainly explainers, reported essays, or personal diaries. They usually do three things at once:

1. Frame an important question or tension.
2. Offer a better way to think about it.
3. Push the reader toward a practical, moral, or strategic conclusion.

The writing should help the reader _orient_, not just inform them.

## Author Stance

- First person is good when it signals judgment or accountability: `I think`, `I worry`, `I suspect`, `it seems to me`, `my guess is`.
- Use `I` to own a view, not to make the piece autobiographical.
- Speak as a careful observer who is trying to get the reader to see the structure of a problem.
- Explicitly acknowledge uncertainty, ambiguity, and missing evidence.
- Do not pretend to have direct access, institutional authority, or technical involvement that we do not actually have.

## Tone

- Earnest, high-stakes, future-facing.
- More persuasive than detached.
- More sober than sensational.
- Often morally charged, but rarely moralizing.
- Comfortable moving between technical, political, and civilizational stakes in the same piece.

Tone can range from measured and formal to sharp and conversational.

## Structure

The writing is usually highly scaffolded. Make the logic easy to follow.

- Open with a tension, misconception, scene, or question that immediately establishes stakes.
- State the thesis early.
- Tell the reader what the piece is trying to do and sometimes what it is _not_ trying to do.
- Use clear section headings for major movements in the argument.
- Let each paragraph do one main job: define, qualify, illustrate, object, or conclude.
- Use numbered or bulleted lists when laying out frameworks, categories, assumptions, or scenarios.
- Favor a progression like:

1. Opening tension and thesis
2. Definitions or framing
3. Mechanism or model of how the world works
4. Concrete examples
5. Objections or caveats
6. Implications
7. Closing synthesis or call to attention

## Paragraph and Sentence Habits

- Write mostly medium-to-long sentences with controlled subclauses.
- Use short sentences to land a point or create contrast.
- Lean on contrast structures: `not X but Y`, `the point is not...`, `the real issue is...`, `on the one hand... on the other hand...`.
- Use parentheticals and qualifying clauses to keep claims precise.
- Repeat key framing phrases across sections when they are doing conceptual work.
- Prefer explicit transitions: `first`, `second`, `however`, `to be clear`, `for example`, `at the same time`, `the point is`, `all of this means`.

The cadence often works like this: qualify, explain, accumulate, then deliver a crisp takeaway.

## Common Rhetorical Moves

- Name a common misunderstanding, then replace it with a better frame.
- Pose a question the reader is likely asking, then answer it immediately.
- Use hypotheticals: `suppose`, `imagine`, `if this is right`, `in that world`.
- Move from abstract claim to concrete example, then back to general implication.
- Concede part of an objection before redirecting.
- Restate the thesis in a sharper way after the evidence has been laid out.
- Tie technical mechanisms back to politics, institutions, incentives, or human consequences.

## Explaining Technical Ideas

- Define specialized terms in plain English as soon as they appear.
- Translate jargon into an intuitive metaphor or everyday analogy.
- Use examples that operationalize the claim instead of merely decorating it.
- When using numbers, use rough order-of-magnitude estimates that make stakes legible.
- Prefer memorable simplifications over exhaustive background, but do not simplify so far that the claim becomes sloppy.

Good pattern:

1. Name the concept
2. Explain it in ordinary language
3. Give one concrete example
4. State why it matters

## Use of Evidence and Sources

Sources are part of the prose, not a separate scholarly apparatus.

- Link phrases naturally inside sentences.
- Name people, papers, institutions, or examples when they add credibility or clarity.
- Use evidence to sharpen a claim that has already been framed, not to dump research onto the reader.
- Prefer a few well-chosen examples over citation overload.
- Quote sparingly. Most of the time, paraphrase and synthesize.
- Use direct quotation only when the wording itself matters.

### No Footnotes

The reference posts often use footnotes for caveats, side arguments, and clarifications. We do **not** want to emulate that.

Instead:

- Put the caveat in the sentence.
- Or add one short follow-up sentence.
- Or use a brief parenthetical.
- Or fold the clarification into the paragraph where the reader needs it.

If a detail is not important enough to survive in the body, cut it.

## Epistemic Style

One of the strongest shared traits is calibrated confidence.

- Make strong directional claims when warranted.
- Hedge details honestly: `likely`, `plausible`, `may`, `might`, `I think`, `I worry`, `I could be wrong`.
- Distinguish between what seems true, what is observable, and what is speculative.
- Admit when evidence is incomplete.
- Avoid false certainty, but also avoid mushy agnosticism.

The ideal effect is: "this author is thinking clearly under uncertainty."

## What the Writing Values

Across the source material, the prose keeps returning to:

- agency
- freedom
- dignity
- coordination
- safety
- capability
- governance
- social consequences of technical systems
- what kinds of futures are worth building or preventing

Even highly technical passages usually resolve into human stakes.

## What Not To Copy

- Insider references that depend on being a lab founder, CEO, researcher, or direct participant.
- Claims of special access unless we truly have it.
- Organization-specific rhetoric or self-justification.
- Footnotes or footnote-like digressions.
- Sci-fi cosplay, prophecy, or messianic language.
- Overheated doom language.
- Academic throat-clearing.
- Snark as the dominant mode.

Occasional bluntness can work in shorter pieces, but it should feel earned and rare.

## Practical Rules for New Essays

- Start with a real question, tension, or misconception.
- State what you believe early.
- Define the key term or frame before building on it.
- Make the argument legible with headings and explicit transitions.
- Use at least one concrete example for every major abstract claim.
- Put caveats in the body, not below the line.
- Quote lightly and link inline.
- End by sharpening the stakes or naming what should follow from the argument.

## Final Check

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does this sound like a person thinking clearly in public, not a brand or a pundit?
2. Is the argument easy to follow at the level of sections, paragraphs, and sentences?
3. Are conviction and uncertainty both visible?
4. Did we translate technical ideas into plain language without flattening them?
5. Are sources integrated inline rather than offloaded to footnotes?
6. Did we avoid borrowed authority and keep the perspective honestly our own?
